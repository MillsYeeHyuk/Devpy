<p><b>Quantum tunnelling</b> or <b>tunneling</b> (see <a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#Doubled_consonants" title="American and British English spelling differences">spelling differences</a>) refers to the <a href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanics" title="Quantum mechanics">quantum mechanical</a> phenomenon where a particle tunnels through a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Potential_barrier" title="Potential barrier">barrier</a> that it <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">classically</a> could not surmount. This plays an essential role in several physical phenomena, such as the <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">nuclear fusion</a> that occurs in <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">main sequence</a> stars like the <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">Sun</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup> It has important <a href="/wiki/Quantum_tunnelling#Applications" title="Quantum tunnelling">applications</a> to modern devices such as the <a href="/wiki/Tunnel_diode" title="Tunnel diode">tunnel diode</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[2]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Quantum_computing" title="Quantum computing">quantum computing</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scanning_tunnelling_microscope" title="Scanning tunnelling microscope">scanning tunnelling microscope</a>. The effect was predicted in the early 20th century and its acceptance as a general physical phenomenon came mid-century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Razavy_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-Razavy-3">[3]</a></sup></p>
